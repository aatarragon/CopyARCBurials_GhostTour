:John Flack Winslow (1858-1877) [Section 65 Lot 39]

:

Construction of the USS Monitor, 5th President of RPI

John Flack Winslow was born in Bennington, Vermont on November 10, 1810, to Captain Richard Winslow and Mary Corning Seymour. He was the fourth of seven sons the couple would have. His family moved to Albany when he was 5.

At 17, he left his schooling and entered the workforce working as a clerk at the W. & A. Marvin Company in Albany. He eventually took a job in a New York City and at 21 left to work for New Jersey Iron Company which started his life-long connection with iron and steel. In 1831 he spent a brief time in New Orleans before returning to New Jersey and in 1833 he learned the process of pig iron at a new job with Bergen and Sussex Iron Company of New Jersey. While working there he met and befriended Erastus Corning who would become his business partner for 30 years.

In 1837 Winslow joined Corning’s Albany Iron Works, and ran the day-to-day operations of the factory while Corning began acquiring the rail lines that would eventually become the New York Central Railroad. Winslow kept his factory competitive by traveling to Europe to gather and purchase cutting edge technology for iron and steel production.

In 1861 Winslow began his most iconic and important work, the construction of the USS Monitor. Winslow funded the ship himself after getting frustrated with the slow bureaucracy of Washington, and was launched quickly with most parts made by Albany Iron Works. The Monitor is credited with saving most of the Union Navy from the Confederate ship the Merrimac.

In 1864 Winslow insured his successful future by purchasing the manufacturing rights to Bessemer Steel. In that same year he also purchased and began construction of Winslow Mortuary Chapel here at Albany Rural Cemetery. The following year, he was made the fifth president of RPI, the first president to not also be a minister. Before that he had been in Poughkeepsie but moved to Troy following his recent change of job.

In the final years of his life he stayed in Poughkeepsie finally dying at age of 82 on March 10, 1892.
