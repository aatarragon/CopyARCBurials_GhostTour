William Topp (1813 -1857) [Section 12, Lot 25]

President of several anti-slavery conventions in New York State, member of Albany’s Underground Railroad, and women’s rights activist

William Topp was born free in Albany to Lewis and Phillis Topp in 1813. It appears they were people of little means, but Lewis was active in and well–respected by the African-American community. We know nothing about William until he first appears in his late 20’s as a political leader. He’s identified as a member of the finance committee of the 1840 New York State Colored Convention held in Albany. By this time, he is co-owner of a men’s tailoring shop and clothing store.

In 1842 Topp he married Eliza Vogelsang from New York City. Through this marriage, he cements his place in both the African American and white political world of anti-slavery activism. Eliza was the daughter of Peter Vogelsang and Maria Miller. Her father was one of the founders of St. Philip’s Episcopal Church in New York, and Thomas Miller, Eliza’s grandfather, was one of the founding members of the A.M. Zion Church in NYC. Both men were founders of the New York African Mutual Relief Society. By 1840 the Miller and Vogelsang families were part of African-American political and social aristocracy of New York City.

The importance of this marriage can’t be under-estimated. It’s unlikely that Peter Vogelsang would have sanctioned marriage to just anyone. Jane, Eliza’s older sister, married James Forten, Jr. in 1838. James Forten, Sr. had served in the Revolution and came to be one of the wealthiest men in Philadelphia of either race. He’s befriended William Lloyd Garrison, funded the publication of Garrison’s The Liberator, and was one of the founders of the American Anti-Slavery Society, the dominant abolitionist organization in the North.

Over the next 15 years, William Topp became the wealthiest African-American in Albany. In 1845 he opened his own business as a merchant tailor and was enormously successful. Business reports over a decade say: “without means, he had made money, retains all his customers,” “does the most fashionable business in the city,” “industrious, attentive,” “frugal habits,” and “very aristocratic.” During this time, Eliza’s younger brother Thomas moves up from New York City to work in the shop.

Topp continued to be an important part of the Albany Colored Citizens Committee, and a trustee of the Albany’s African Baptist Church. He’s a member of the Albany Underground Railroad (UGRR), and in the 1850s, he a publisher, along with Stephen Myers, who is the head of the local UGRR, of the newspaper, The Telegraph and Temperance Journal.

But his activity transcends the city, and he begins an almost meteoric political career. Topp served on important committees of the annual national and state “colored” and anti-slavery conventions in Philadelphia, Boston, and Ohio, and served as President of several New York conventions.

Topp becomes good friends with Gerritt Smith, the wealthy abolitionist politician, and philanthropist, a leader in the New York Anti-slavery Society and founder of the Liberty Party (the only political party in the country devoted solely to the elimination of slavery). He’s also a major voice in the American Anti-Slavery Society, headed by William Lloyd Garrison.

He’s close to Lydia and Abigail Mott, Quaker sisters who were part of Albany’s UGG and dear friends of Frederick Douglass. After Abigail’s death in 1850, the Topp family embraces Lydia, and through Lydia, he comes to know her best friend, Susan B Anthony. Topp becomes one of the few African-American men, along with Frederick Douglass (with whom he became very close), to take up the issue of women’s suffrage.

The Library of Congress (LOC) contains an amazing artifact – an inscribed copy of “Uncle Tom’s Cabin” given to Lydia Mott by Topp in 1853. Lydia, 20 years later – just before her death, gave her treasured copy to Susan B. Anthony. When Anthony donated the book to the LOC, Anthony writes a note in it in which she refers to William Topp as “a splendid man.”

In December 1857, Topp’s brief, but remarkable, life ended. For many months he had been suffering from tuberculosis; he died at the age of 44. Aaron Powell, a Quaker abolitionist from Ghent, Columbia County, wrote a notice of Topp’s death that appeared in The Liberator, “Few there are whose lives have been more uniformly and so religiously consecrated to labor for the promotion of the best interests and well-being of their fellow man.”

Many of the bodies interred within Section 12 Lot 25 were originally buried elsewhere in Albany, but later moved to Albany Rural Cemetery at the end of the 1870s.

In total, 13 individuals are buried within the Topp family plot. Some of them include William (d.1857); his wife Eliza (d.1865); his son Alfred (d. 1854); his parents Lewis (d. 1838) and Phillis (d. 1853); daughter Blanche (1858); daughter Mary (1884) and son Lewis (1912).

Also buried in the plot are William’s sister Mary Topp Barquet (1880); sister-in-law and wife of James Forten Jr. , Jane Vogelsang Forten (d. 1852); and their daughter Maria Forten (d. 1847). Another sister-in-law, Rebecca Bishop Vogelsang and her son Alfred (d. 1854), are also buried within the plot. (Rebecca came from one of the wealthiest and most respected African-American families in Annapolis, Maryland.)
