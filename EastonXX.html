Charles Patterson Easton (1824 – 1885) [Section 27 Lot 11]

Founder of C.P. Easton & Co. Lumber Company, President of the Board of Public Instruction which established Albany High School, Sat on commission which oversaw construction of Albany City Hall, Sunday school teacher at Methodist Episcopal Church

Charles Patterson Easton was born in Albany on October 10, 1824. He was the only child to Ephraim Easton and Eliza Patterson, who were both of Scots-Irish decedent. Eliza was married previously in Belfast before immigrating to Montreal in 1818, giving birth to a daughter, Eliza, in 1819, with her first husband dying a month after giving birth. Ephraim came to Montreal in 1818, with both of his parents dying within the first year of arrival. They were married on January 24, 1824, and moved to Albany shortly after traveling by bobsled.

Charles received a formal education at Albany Academy. At age 14 he began working in Albany’s lumber district, first as a tally boy, later given more responsibility as the years went by. In 1847, at age 23 he established his own lumber business, C. P. Easton & Company.

On January 26, 1847, he married Mary Boyd, daughter of Jesse Conde Boyd, a lumber manufacturer in Albany. Like Charles, she had a formal education, attending Albany Female Academy. Additionally, she is a decedent of Walter Fitz-Alan, First High Steward of Scotland to King David I. Together they had nine children.

By 1857, C. P. Easton & Company had become one of the most successful and largest wholesale lumber dealers in the area, dealing almost exclusively in Canada pine. The company made a yearly profit of over $300,000 (almost $6 million in 2019). As Charles’ sons got older, he made them partners, and by 1872 the company was also referred to as C.P. Easton & Son.

Between the years of 1865 – 1881 Charles served as a member of the Board of Public Instruction, which advances public education. Seven of those years he served as the president and authored as a resolution for the creation of Albany Free Academy, later referred to as Albany High School. The resolution was adopted in July of 1867. Though there was opposition, Charles secured appropriations and opened the following year with 141 students. By 1873 the Academy has grown too large for the rented space, on State Street and a permanent building was completed in 1876 on Clinton Avenue on what is today the Albany County Family Courthouse. With this new space, Albany Free Academy’s name was changed to Albany High School. During this period of transition, the Wilberforce School which had been operated since 1857 to accommodate “colored children” ceased to exist when it was integrated into Albany schools.

Charles became interested in politics being an early convert to the Republican Party upon its formation. In 1872 he ran for a seat in the New York State Assembly and the following years New York State Senate; he was unsuccessful every time. He served on the Republican committee, serving as president for one term. He was frequently a delegate to the New York State Republican Convention and was a delegate to the 1880 Republican National Convention in Chicago. At the convention, Charles was one of the 306 votes cast to nominate Ulysses Grant for a third term. Though Garfield got the nomination, Charles received a bronze medal commemorating the event.

Charles’ civic work went beyond public education; he served as director of the National Exchange Bank; trustee to Albany Orphan Asylum; president of Young Men’s Association; and a charter member and trustee of the Fort Orange Club. He was also a devote Christian, serving as a Sunday school teacher for the Methodist Episcopal Church, and manager of the Albany County Bible Society. In 1878, the state legislature appointed him to a commission which sought to enlarge Clinton Prison in the Village of Dannemora. Two years later, in 1880, he was appointed to a commission which oversaw the construction of the new Albany City Hall. The same year Governor Alonzo Cornell appointed him as military staff, Quartermaster General and Brigadier General.

In January 1885, Charles went on an extended holiday throughout the South. However, upon arriving in St. Augustine, Florida, he became ill and died on March 3, 1885. He was 60 years old. He is buried in the Easton family plot at Albany Rural Cemetery. A carving of him is immortalized in the State Capitol Building “Million Dollar Staircase” also known as the Great Western Staircase. The engraving is located on the third floor. After his death, a memorial for was voted on unanimously by the faculty of Albany High School Faculty:

In Memoriam. Gen. Charles P. Easton

One triumph his, some held it won in vain,

Some kept aloof, some seeing not its fruits

Still interposed the bar of hostile speech,

A mental vineyard planted, watches and held;

No selfish fruitage planning to himself,

But making free to all, that ever came,

As sun and rain, the broad and pleasant slopes.

He was a friend to Learning - this his crown;

A friend to youth - here springs his monument.

They mark, believe, revere, enshrine the years

Wherewith time clad his brows with reverent snows.
