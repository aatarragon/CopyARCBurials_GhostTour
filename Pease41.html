Brevet Major Charles Elliott Pease (1838-1886) [Section 41 Lot 21]

Civil War Officer. Carried Gen. Robert E. Lee’s “3rd Letter” to Gen. U. S. Grant requesting terms of surrender. Rode with Grant and Lee on the same day during the Civil War.

Charles Elliott Pease was born on August 16, 1838 in Albany. He was one of nine children born to Richard Henry Pease and Mary Elliott Dawes Pease. Charles graduated from the Albany Academy and went on to attend Union College in Schenectady for two years. He left Union College to work for his father, owner of the Excelsior Agricultural Works Warehouse and Seed Store on State Street in Albany. The company sold farm implements and seed.

During this time, Col. Elmer Ellsworth, a native of Ballston Spa, transformed the National Guard Cadets of Chicago into the United States Zouave Cadets in 1859. He was responsible for popularizing the Zouave style which originated in France. The unique drill of the Zouaves was Franco-Algerian in nature. The uniform of the enlisted man was characterized by an open-front waist jacket, red chasseur trousers, white gaiters, yellow waist sashes and red fez hats.

After a performance of the U.S. Zouave Cadets in Washington Park in Albany, the city fathers decided that Albany should have their own Zouave organization and the Albany Zouave Cadets were formed in 1861. Charles Pease joined the unit in June of that year. The Albany Zouave Cadets comprised Company A of the 10th NYS Militia. Leaving the 10th NYS Militia, Charles Pease went on to enlist in the 44th NY Volunteer Infantry Regiment in September 1861 and was mustered in as first lieutenant of Company G. The 44th was known as the Ellsworth Avengers. Elmer Ellsworth was the first Union officer killed in the Civil War after removing a Confederate Flag from the pole atop the Marshall House in Alexandria, Virginia.

In 1863 Pease was transferred to the staff of Brig. Gen. James H. Van Alen (a native of Kinderhook) in the 3rd NY Cavalry Regiment as Van Alen’s aide-de-camp. Pease was promoted to captain and Assistant Adjutant General, U.S. Volunteers. He was then appointed to the staff of Maj. Gen. Joseph Hooker, and subsequently Maj. Gen. George G. Meade, both commanders of the Union Army of the Potomac. About six weeks into this latest assignment, Capt. Pease would arrive on the field of the Battle of Gettysburg, where he had a horse shot out from under him. In August 1864, Gen. Meade rewarded Capt. Pease with a promotion to Brevet Major for “faithful and meritorious service in the field.” Later, at the Battle of Hatcher’s Run in February of 1865 during the Appomattox Campaign, Pease had another horse killed while he was in the saddle.

It was on the morning of April 9, 1865 when Confederate Lt. Gen. Robert E. Lee’s third letter, which requested Union Lt. Gen. Ulysses S. Grant to discuss terms of surrender, was received by Gen. George G. Meade. Meade gave the letter to Maj. Pease to carry the message to Gen. Grant. Upon reading the letter, Grant instructed Pease to remain in Grant’s company as he rode to Appomattox Court House. Maj. Pease was invited into the Wilmer McLean House where the surrender papers were signed. Gen. Grant introduced his staff to Gen. Lee, and included Charles E. Pease in his introduction.

After the surrender was signed, Gen. Grant directed Charles Pease to provide a military escort to Gen. Lee back to Lee’s headquarters tent as a military courtesy. In doing so, Bvt. Maj. Charles Pease had the honor to ride with Gen. U.S. Grant and Gen. Robert E. Lee. on the same day during the Civil War.

Pease submitted his resignation from the army just six days after the surrender and returned to Albany, where he married his sweetheart, Catherine “Kitty” Gansevoort Trotter in 1866. When Pease accepted a position with the United States Life Insurance Company, the couple moved to New York City. Charles and Kitty had one child, a daughter, Estelle Cuyler Pease. She would die at only 15 months old of chicken pox.

Charles E. Pease died on March 25, 1886 at the age of 48. Cause of death was diabetes. He was buried in the Trotter family plot next to his daughter, Estelle. His headstone bears no reference whatsoever of his honorable and faithful military service to his country.
