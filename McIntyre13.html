Archibald McIntyre (1772 – 1858) [Section 9 Lot 5]

State Comptroller, NYS Assemblyman, NYS Senator, Deputy Secretary of State, Owner of Adirondack Iron and Steel Company, namesake to MacIntyre Mountains west of Mt. Marcy

Archibald McIntyre was born on June 1, 1772, in Kenmore, Perthshire, Scotland, and the son of Daniel McIntyre and Ann Walker. Two years later his family immigrated to America. Not much is known about his formative years, he worked in the public sector is record serving in many elected and appointed positions during the early years of New York State. He served as Deputy Secretary of State from 1801 – 1806, as well as New York State Comptroller from 1806 – 1821. He held these positions while also holding a position in the state Assembly from from Montgomery County, first in 1798 and several times until 1821.

As Comptroller he expanded the functions of the position to include banking, canals, health, defense, and taxes. He set the tone of the position to be both professional and nonpartisan of the state-wide office. However, he became a harsh critic of Governor Tompkins financial impropriety due to war-related funds. His voice concerns on Tompkins viewed as the main reason he lost reelection, McIntryre was later removed from the position shortly thereafter.

From 1822 – 1826 McIntyre served as a member of the New York State Senate, first in the Middle District. At the time Senate Districts were divided into four districts around the state: Southern, Middle, Eastern and Western. From 1823 onward he represented the Fourth Senate District which included Clinton, Essex, Franklin, Hamilton, Montgomery, St. Lawrence, Saratoga, Warren, and Washington Counties.

In the mid-1830s he and his brother-in-law established the Elba Iron and Steel Manufacturing Company in North Elba, also known as the Adirondack Iron and Steel Company. Initially searching for silver, they discovered iron ore and started excavation in 1838. Because of all the heat required to melt the iron ore the area was clear-cut. The company established several operations in the area of Mt. Marcy, an Upper Works was set up in the Town of Newcomb, and a company town was established. Known as McIntyre, today referred to as Tahawus. On September 3, 1845, McIntyre’s son-in-law David Henderson, his 10-year-old son and a guide went into the woods to look for alternative sources of water for the iron ore mines. Henderson was responsible for the mines. During the hike his rifle accidentally discharged and shot him, resulting in his death. The pond where the incident took place was later be renamed Calamity Pond, with a marker, the only one of its kind within the Adirondack State Park.

Due to impurities of the iron ore in the Adirondacks, the processing was very difficult. Additionally, the lack of a railroad nearby made costs of transport extremely high, resulting in slower production. The company ceased operations in 1857.

In addition to his iron ore business venture, McIntyre partnered with Henry Yates, brother to Governor Joseph Yates, on various projects throughout the early nineteeth-century. From 1821 – 1834 they served as operators for the New York State Lottery, as well as lotteries in other states. In May of 1842, they purchase the Ithaca and Owego Railroad in a public sale, later renaming it the Cayuga and Susquehanna Railroad. They sold the company in 1849, which later become a part of the Delaware, Lackawanna and Western Railroad.

McIntyre died on May 6, 1858, and buried in the McIntyre family plot, having secured an early spot in the cemetery serving as one of the first trustees. The MacIntrye Mountains in the Adirondacks are named in his honor.
