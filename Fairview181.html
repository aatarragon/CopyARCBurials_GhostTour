Fairview Home for the Friendless [Section 104 Lot 453]

Group burial plot for children from a working farm and orphanage 1888 - 1956

Located on Boght Road, the former Wiswall Road, near the corner of Haswell Road and St. Colman’s School, sat the Fairview Home for the Friendless, an orphanage and a working farm within the current Town of Colonie.

It was established by James Barclay Jermain, a lawyer who managed his father’s estate. His father, Sylvanus Jermain, was a successful merchant and served as the first secretary of Albany Savings Bank, and a founder of the Mechanics and Farmer’s Bank. Upon his father’s death in 1869, James inherited his father’s fortune and home, Hedge Laws, which was originally built by William Jenkins Worth, while overseeing the Watervliet Arsenal. Worth later became General for the Union Troops in the American-Mexican Conflict and is namesake to Ft. Worth, Texas.

Jermain financed several projects in the Albany area, including the Jermain Memorial Church (Jermain Presbyterian Church) in Watervliet; founded the Home for Aged Men in Menands; and the YMCA on the corner of Steuben Place and North Pearl Street which had one of the first indoor pools in the country.

An offshoot of the Mohawk and Hudson River Humane Society, which was founded in 1887, “for the temporary care of delinquent children in Albany and Rensselaer counties.” In 1888 the Fairview Home for Friendless Children was a private corporation that cared for friendless, destitute, and committed children for both boys and girls under the original name Albany County Society for the Prevention of Cruelty to Children.

The Fairview Home was said to be situated on the highest point in the Town of Watervliet (at the time) which is noted in the David Addison Harsha’s 1891 book Noted Living Albanians and State Officials: A series of Biographical Sketches, “with commanding a fine view of East Troy, West Troy, a portion of Cohoes, Lansingburgh, Oakwood cemetery and the Hudson River for several miles. The location was delightful, having the Hudson River visible in the whole vicinity. A large front yard, with beautiful maple and elm trees, adds greatly to the attractions of the place. The grounds belonging to the institution contain fifty-six acres, thirteen of which are reserved, and the remainder rented to a practical farmer who occupies a cottage on the premises. It is expected, however, that in time, the whole land will be worked by the inmates of the home.”

The working farm and orphanage had several amenities in addition to the main building. The property included several crop fields, a separate play area for boys and girls, ball field, hospital, root cellar, pigpen, ice house, a barn which included a milk room and stalls for horse and cows, chicken coop, and a sunken area which was flooded in the winter for ice skating.

James’ daughter, Maria, served as treasurer for both the Fairview Home as well as the Mohawk and Hudson River Humane Society. The Humane Society evolved its mission and in 1894, the state Legislature passed a law that changed the name to the Mohawk and Hudson River Humane Society for the Prevention of Cruelty to Animals.

The same year, on November 15, 1894, a group plot was purchased at Albany Rural Cemetery for $1. The first burial was Lillie Faulkner, age 7, on July 12, 1893, which predates the purchase of the plot. The next was Herman Docke in 1901, Julien Cowan (1901), Freda Glaser, May 8, 19015, Florence M Daley, May 11, 1905, Hawley Hudson, April 22, 1908, St. John F. Smith, May 5, 1908, and Walter Pike, age, 10, April 26, 1910. Though there were eight burials, one of the boys was reinterred with his mother at a later date.
