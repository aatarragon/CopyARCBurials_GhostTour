Douglas Lodge (1842 - 1863) [Section 40, Lot 46

Civil War. Union Army Officer, Captain of Co. A, 43rd NY Infantry. Killed during the Second Battle of Fredericksburg, Virginia.

According to family records, Douglas Lodge was born in Albany, New York in 1842 (although his roster information states that he was 21 years of age when he enlisted in 1861, which would make his date of birth in 1840). Douglas was the son of Benjamin Lodge, a tailor, and Elizabeth Douglas Lodge. His parents came to this country from Scotland in the Spring of 1843. Douglas was educated in a city public primary school and at Albany Academy. At a young age, he developed an interest in the Navy, and received an appointment to the United States Naval Academy at Annapolis, Maryland. After a year at the Naval Academy he became disenfranchised with the “monotony and overbearing spirit of students from the Southern States.” After returning to Albany, he engaged in commercial businesses.

Soon after the Confederate attack on Ft. Sumter in Charleston Harbor, South Carolina, President Abraham Lincoln called for seventy-five thousand volunteers to put down the Rebellion. Douglas Lodge was one of the first to offer his services to his country, enlisting in Co. B of the 25th NYS Militia in April 1861. The 25th NYSM was assigned to Arlington Heights, across the Potomac River in Virginia. The regiment soon commenced the erection of their fortification, which they named Fort Albany. It was built almost entirely by the men of the 25th. In August Lodge enlisted in the 43rd New York Volunteer Infantry Regiment, known as the “Albany Rifles” and was mustered in as Third Sergeant in Co. A. During the Peninsula Campaign of 1862, Lodge was promoted to Quartermaster Sergeant, and received subsequent promotions to Lieutenant and ultimately Captain of his Company. In his letters to his father, he wrote of the ultimate successes of the army in putting down the rebellion.

On May 2, 1863, Capt. Lodge was struck on the belt buckle by shrapnel from a Confederate cannon ball. He was stunned but received no serious physical injury. Then, on May 4th at the Second Battle of Fredericksburg, Virginia, during the Chancellorsville Campaign, while commanding his company during a skirmish with the enemy, Capt. Lodge was wounded in action, shot by a Minié ball in the left temple. Amazingly, Lodge was found still alive. He was removed him from the field of battle, but the wound proved mortal, and he passed away the next day, May 5, 1863. His corpse was embalmed and sent to his family at 151 Clinton Avenue in Albany.

Captain Lodge was only twenty-one years old when he died. His funeral from the Unitarian Church on Division Street in Albany was largely attended. Initially, Lodge’s remains were deposited in a public receiving vault in the old State St. Burial grounds, now the site of Washington Park. This occurred possibly from the high number of war casualties being returned to Albany. Capt. Douglas Lodge was laid to rest in his family plot on the South Ridge, although the date of burial is not recorded. Sadly, his mother would not recover from sickness and passed away the following February. Capt. Douglas Lodge is buried in Section 40, Lot 46 in Albany Rural Cemetery Menands, NY.
