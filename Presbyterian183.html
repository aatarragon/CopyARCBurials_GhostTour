United Presbyterian Church [Section 49 Lot 8]

Group plot of first United Presbyterian Church in the United States

The first United Presbyterian Church in the United States was located in Albany on the corner of Sheridan Street and Chapel Street. Those of Scottish ancestry and other European groups who were Calvinists found religious service in Albany’s Dutch Reformed Church. However, given the language and culture based on Dutch-American, there was a growing desire to establish a meeting housing of their own. By the 1760s, English Presbyterians through the Presbyterian Synod of Philadelphia served the city by occasional preachers.

By 1762 land was granted to the area known as “the gallows hill” on Hudson and Grand Street, just west of South Pearl Street, where the Times Union Center Stands. With the onset of the Revolutionary War, services were suspended as congregates were inherently British sympathizers.

The Presbyterian Church reopened in 1784, which was independent of any British connection. John McDonald, who was ordained within the Church of Scotland, served as the first minister after the war. However, he vehemently opposed what he considered radical modern trends of the church, that being singing hymns by Wesley and Watts that was gaining popularity, wherein he viewed proper praise in the metric version of the Psalms.

This rift led McDonald and a band of followers to separate from the church. From 1795 – 1800, they met in private homes until the following year when McDonald was received into the Presbytery of Montreal and formed a new church, the United Presbyterian Church of Albany. The new church aligned themselves under the Canadian Church rather than affiliate with other Albany groups who were part of the Scottish Covenanters. It was not until 1858 when the organizations received national recognition.

The City of Albany granted the United Presbyterians a location on the corner of Canal (Sheridan) Street and Chapel Street. Built in the Federal-style in 1801, it served as a place of worship until 1861, which during the years of the Civil War, its preacher, Dr. Samuel Morrow, made the church a popular location as he was a nationally recognized abolitionist who denounced slavery from the pulpit.

Seeking a larger location on the “Fifth Avenue of Albany,” the United Presbyterian Church erected a new home at 8 Lancaster Street, but by 1899 sold it to the Unitarian Church. Their new home of worship, Park Church, was located at Western and South Lake Avenue for more than 60 years.

In 1958 the Park Church merged with the Presbyterian Church USA and was known as Park United Presbyterian Church, forming the United Fourth Presbyterian Church. As its congregants moved into the suburbs throughout the 1960s and 1970s, their current location is at 916 Western Avenue under the auspice of the New Covenant Presbyterian Church.

The original location for the United Presbyterian Church was in Sheridan Hollow. It was torn down in the early 1960s, which at the time was known as the Times-Union garage, a mechanic shop, located next to the Times-Union building.

On December 21, 1868, The Trustees of the United Presbyterian Church, Albany NY purchased a group plot in Albany Rural Cemetery for $1, as the State Street Burial Ground was being converted into Washington Park. The United Presbyterian section was situated between the Presbyterians and Roman Catholics, and between State and Lancaster Street. The first burial took place on April 16, 1874, for Adam Ramsay, who was 32 years old. The last reinternment occurred for Julia Ann Wise, age five years, one month, and 25 days, who died on December 10, 1842. There are roughly 100 individuals of the United Presbyterian Church of Albany buried in this group plot. Seventy three headstones exist in the United Presbyterian lot.
