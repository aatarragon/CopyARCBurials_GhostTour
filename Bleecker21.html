Johannes Bleecker, Jr. (1668 - 1738) [ Section 49 Lot 1 ]

8th Mayor of Albany, carried captive to Canada by French and Indians, member of Colonial General Assembly.

Johannes Bleecker Jr. was born in 1668 to parents Jan Jansen Bleeker and his wife, Margaret Van Woert Bleecker. One of 9 children, he was the oldest son, having 5 sisters and 3 brothers. As a family, they occupied a home on Pearl Street. The family were members of the Albany Dutch Church where his father Jan Jansen served as deacon and church elder.

Following in his father’s footsteps, Johannes Jr. entered into the fur trade, often venturing into the Indian country on his father’s behalf. During the winter of 1686-87, the now eighteen-year-old accompanied Patrick Mc Gregory and a party far into the wilderness to establish trade with the Ottawa’s. Seized by the French and Seneca’s, he was imprisoned in Montreal for a year, until the French governor granted his release. During those years he became fluent in native languages to be distinguished as an Indian interpreter.

In 1693, he married Albany native Anna Coster, thereafter settling into a home on the Northwest corner of Pearl Street and Maiden Lane. At the time, Pearl Street was home to the Lansings, Bleeckers, Cuylers, Rosebooms, and other prominent early Albany families. Over the next twenty-five years, their nine children were baptized in the Albany Dutch church where both parents continued their membership.

Johannes Jr. continued trading, and sought to develop beyond the legacy cast by his prominent father. His ambition was revealed by his willingness to accept patronage from the insurgent/pretender Jacob Leisler. Jacob Leisler was a German-born colonist. He gained wealth in New Amsterdam (later New York City) in the fur trade and tobacco business. Beginning in 1689, Leisler led an insurrection and seized control of the city by taking over Fort James at the lower end of Manhattan. Then, he assumed control and appointed himself as acting Lieutenant Governor of the Province of New York. Leisler was arrested by the newly appointed governor of New York in March 1691, condemned and executed in New York City for treason against the English crown monarchs.

Despite Johannes Jr’s impetuous behavior, he was able to establish himself as an Albany pillar. During the 1690s, Johannes Jr. served on Albany’s city council as well as an officer in the militia. Already groomed for leadership, the provincial governor appointed him recorder (Deputy Mayor) of the city in 1700. The next year, he succeeded his father Jan Jansen as mayor of Albany, serving for one year. During his term as Mayor, the city wall was strengthened expecting an attack from the French.

In 1701, he was chosen to represent Albany in the provincial Assembly, serving for a year. Later, he was an Albany property assessor and then census taker for the Five Nations. Although retired from public life, in 1720 he was appointed to the Commissioners of Indian Affairs.

Johannes Bleecker, Jr. died on December 20, 1738 and was buried in the Dutch church cemetery. His younger brother, Rutger, became mayor of Albany in 1726. His remains now rest in the Albany rural Cemetery Section 49 Lot 1 (Dutch Church – Churchgrounds).
