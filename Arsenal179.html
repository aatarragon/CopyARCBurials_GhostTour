Arsenal Burial Ground [Section 100 Lot 28]

Burial Ground for the Gibbonsville Reformed Church in Watervliet

Unlike many of Albany Rural Cemetery’s oldest headstones which were transferred from the State Street Burying Ground or elsewhere in the city, the Arsenal burying ground was transferred from Watervliet. The city of Watervliet has gone through several names changes throughout history, Port Schuyler, Washington, Gibbonsville, and West-Troy.

On January 1, 1805, James Gibbons purchased land from John S. Schuyler, establishing a village. The area already had about 15 households and included a bell and brass foundry owned by Julius Hanks, which was later sold to Andrew Meneely. On July 14, 1813, Gibbons sold twelve acres to the United States government for the establishment of the Watervliet Arsenal. The following year, on March 14, 1814, the Reformed Protestant Dutch Church of Washington and Gibbonsville was established.

Nearly a century later, the church's cemetery, which contained between 275 and 300 graves, was all but abandoned. The Watervliet Arsenal was looking to expand its operation and sought to acquire the land on which the burying grounds were located, in the southwest corner of the property. In 1913 Reverend C.P. Evans undertook an initiative to copy the headstone inscriptions from each grave, as the site had long been abandoned for burial purposes and was in a state of gross neglect.

A 1919 transcript of the Subcommittee on House Committee of Appropriations, Sundry Civil Appropriation Bill indicates a request from Congress for $15,000 which include purchase of the land, and the removal and reinternment of the bodies, at $20 per body in the hopes of permitting direct access to the new shops of the Delaware & Hudson Railroad.

The bodies and gravestones were removed and reinterred in Albany Rural Cemetery’s North Ridge in 1918. Though some of the stones were reinstalled upright, a majority were laid flat and have been reclaimed by the earth. On Sept 17, 1959, “unknown human bones” found at the Arsenal were brought to the cemetery to be reinterred.

Among those relocated was Benjamin Hanks, a Revolutionary War veteran as well as a relative and key figure of the area’s bell-making industry. One interesting occupant of the Arsenal Burying Ground who was not reburied at the Rural Cemetery was Doctor Nelson L. Hungerford. On May 27, 1839, he was killed by a falling rock in the Cave of the Winds at Niagara Falls. Originally buried at the Arsenal, he was later re-interred in Connecticut.
