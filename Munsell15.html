Joel Munsell Jr. (1808-1880)[Section 4, Lot 47]

Printer, Scholar, Antiquarian, Writer, and author of Annals of Albany

Joel Munsell, Jr. was born on April 14, 1808, in Northfield, Massachusetts, to Cynthia Paine, and Joel Munsell, a wheelwright, one who repairs wooden wheels. At a young age, Joel became an apprentice to a printer in Greenfield, MA, eventually running the shop. By 1826 he moved to Troy, later relocating to Albany the following year to work for a book dealer. At the shop, he worked as a clerk, eventually becoming a manager. In his spare time, he established a subscription publication, The Minerva, which had 150 members.

Both of these jobs were short-lived as he went to work for a newspaper for six years before establishing a printing business. Following his growing passion for printing, in 1836, he bought a printing firm at 58 State Street. His “bread and butter” clients were primarily school groups, religious organizations, libraries and civic societies, which include Albany Daily Statesman, Odd Fellows’ Journal, and abolitionist papers such The Northern Star, and The Freeman’s Advocate.

At the same time, his increasing interest in the history of the New York State and early Americana, he began printing historical pamphlets. He printed what he referred to as Bibliotheca Munselliana. Some of these include Buryone’s Campaign, History of Schoharie County and Border Wars, The Mechanics’ Mirror, and Munsell’s Guide to the Hudson River by Railroad and Steamboat. In 1861 he printed the 200 copies for the American edition of Richard de Bury’s 1345 Philobiblon, a collection of essays that discuss acquisition, preservation, and organization of books. From 1849 – 1859, he published his most well-known and highly referred historical pieces Annals of Albany a 10-volume series of the Albany area.

On June 17, 1834, he married Jane Caroline Bigelow of Albany. They had four children together. She passed away in 1854 and is buried in Albany Rural Cemetery. Two years later, Munsell married Mary Ann Reid of Montreal and have six children together.

Munsell served as a founding member of the Member of Albany Institute of Art and History, and in 1854 was elected to the American Antiquarian Society. Additionally, he served on many historical societies throughout the United States.

Joel Munsell, Jr. died in 1880 at the age of 71 and is buried alongside both of his wives. The granite marker is modest compared to the work he did for the City of Albany. Upon his death, a bronze tablet was erected outside his print shop, which was later taken over by one of his sons. The tablet read “did more than any other man to preserve ancient records of his adopted city.” The tablet is lost to history, as the building was sold in 1899 and demolished to make way for the National Commerce Bank.
