Pvt. Terrence Begley (1844-1864) [Section 100A Lot 100 Row 2 Grave 39 ]

Medal of Honor Recipient, 7th NY Heavy Artillery regiment, served during the battles of Spottsylvania, North Anna, and Cold Harbor.

Terrence Begley was born in Ireland in 1844. He immigrated to America with his family at a young age; they settled in Albany, New York.

In February 1864 Terrence Begley enlisted as a private in the 7th NY Heavy Artillery regiment. He was assigned to Company D. This unit was originally an infantry regiment also known as the “Albany County Regiment” or “Seymour Guard”. In mid-December 1862, the regiment was converted into a heavy artillery contingent.

Four months after enlistment his regiment was embroiled in the 1864 Battle of Cold Harbor. During the battle he shot a Confederate color bearer from the 26th Virginia Infantry, then rushed forward and seized the Confederate colors. While exposed to heavy fire, Terrence returned to the union lines safely. Pvt. Begley was commended by his superiors for valor and recommended for the Medal of Honor.

Months after the engagement Congress approved Pvt. Begley's Medal of Honor application. The medal itself was received on December 1, 1864 at his regiment's field headquarters. However, there was to be no presentation ceremony, as Pvt. Begley had been killed in the Second Battle of Reams Station during the Siege of Petersburg, Virginia, on August 25, 1864. The award was posthumous and the actual Medal was returned to Washington.

Begley’s body was initially buried in a mass grave on the battlefield by Confederate soldiers who had overrun the field. As the soldiers could not identify the Union casualties, burials were made in shallow unmarked graves. Some time later, all remains were disinterred and relocated. Pvt.Begley rests in the Poplar Grove National Cemetery in Petersburg,Virginia among his comrades in unidentified graves.

In the Albany Rural Cemetery, Pvt. Begley's grave marker is located in the Grand Army of the Republic Lot, Section 100A Lot 100, Row 2 Grave 39. His marker is a fitting tribute to one of Albany’s bravest young men who was awarded the nation’s highest military honor.
