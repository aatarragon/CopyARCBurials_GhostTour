Charles Patterson Easton (1824 – 1885) [Section 27 Lot 11]

Founder of C.P. Easton & Co. Lumber Company, President of the Board of Public Instruction which established Albany High School, Sat on commission which oversaw construction of Albany City Hall, Sunday school teacher at Methodist Episcopal Church

Charles Patterson Easton was born in Albany on October 10, 1824. He was the only child to Ephraim Easton and Eliza Patterson, who were both of Scots-Irish decedent. Eliza was married previously in Belfast before immigrating to Montreal in 1818, giving birth to a daughter in 1819. Eliza's first husband died a month after her giving birth. Ephraim came to Montreal in 1818, with both of his parents dying within a year following their arrival. Ephraim and Eliza were married on January 24, 1824, and moved to Albany shortly thereafter, making the trip by bobsled.

Charles received a formal education at Albany Academy. At age 14 he began working in Albany’s lumber district, first as a tally boy, until given more responsibility. In 1847, at age 23 he established his own lumber business, C. P. Easton & Company.

On January 26, 1847, he married Mary Boyd, daughter of Jesse Conde Boyd, a lumber manufacturer in Albany. Like Charles, she had a formal education, attending Albany Female Academy. Additionally, she was a decedent of Walter Fitz-Alan, First High Steward of Scotland to King David I. Together Charles and Jessie had nine children.

By 1857, C. P. Easton & Company had become one of the largest and most successful lumber wholesalers in the area, dealing almost exclusively in Canadian pine. The company recorded annual profits of over $300,000 (comparable to $6 million in 2019 dollars). As Charles’ sons became of age they were made business partners. By 1872 the company was referred to as C.P. Easton & Sons.

Between the years of 1865 – 1881 Charles served as a member of the Board of Public Instruction. During his seven years as board President, he authored a resolution for the creation of the Albany Free Academy. The resolution was adopted in July of 1867. Although there was opposition, Charles secured appropriations, allowing the school to open the following year with 141 students. By 1873 the Academy has grown too large for the rented space on State Street. Construction of a permenant building was completed three years later on Clinton Avenue; today's site of the Albany County Family Courthouse. With this new space, (pictured at Right) Albany Free Academy’s name was changed to Albany High School. During this period of transition, the Wilberforce School which had been operated since 1857 to accommodate “colored children” ceased to exist, and was incorporated into Albany's public schools.

Charles became interested in politics being an early convert to the Republican Party upon its formation. In 1872 he ran for a seat in the New York State Assembly, and the following years New York State Senate; his campaigns were unsuccessful. He served on the Republican committee, serving as president for one term. He was frequently a delegate to the New York State Republican Convention and was a delegate to the 1880 Republican National Convention in Chicago. At the convention, Charles was one of the 306 votes cast to nominate Ulysses Grant for a third term. Though Garfield got the nomination, Charles received a bronze medal commemorating the event.

Charles’ civic work went beyond public education; he served as director of the National Exchange Bank; trustee to Albany Orphan Asylum; president of Young Men’s Association; and a charter member and trustee of the Fort Orange Club. He was also a devote Christian, serving as a Sunday school teacher for the Methodist Episcopal Church, and manager of the Albany County Bible Society. In 1878, the state legislature appointed him to a commission which sought to enlarge Clinton Prison in the Village of Dannemora. Two years later, in 1880, he was appointed to a commission which oversaw the construction of the new Albany City Hall. The same year Governor Alonzo Cornell appointed him as military staff, Quartermaster General and Brigadier General.

In January 1885, Charles went on an extended holiday throughout the Southern United States. Upon arriving in St. Augustine, Florida, he became ill and died on March 3, 1885, at age 60. He is buried in the Easton family plot at Albany Rural Cemetery. He is immortalized by a carving in the State Capitol Building's “Million Dollar Staircase” (Image at Left) also known as the Great Western Staircase. The engraving is located on the third floor. After his death, a memorial was unanimously approved by the faculty of Albany High School to wit:

In Memoriam. Gen. Charles P. Easton

One triumph his, some held it won in vain,

Some kept aloof, some seeing not its fruits

Still interposed the bar of hostile speech,

A mental vineyard planted, watches and held;

No selfish fruitage planning to himself,

But making free to all, that ever came,

As sun and rain, the broad and pleasant slopes.

He was a friend to Learning - this his crown;

A friend to youth - here springs his monument.

They mark, believe, revere, enshrine the years

Wherewith time clad his brows with reverent snows.
