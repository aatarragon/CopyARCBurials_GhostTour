Catherine Mary "Mary" Williams (1833-1884) [Section 99, Lot 3]

African American Vice President of the Albany Women’s Suffrage Society, and teacher in Albany's segragated Wilberforce School and for the Freedman's Bureau in the South after the Civil War

More Info on Catherine Mary Williams & Womens Suffrage.

Catherine Mary Douge was born in 1833 to Susan and Michael Douge, a barber, in Albany, NY. Her parents were leaders of the African community. Michael was in the middle of everything that affected the Black community socially and politically, advocating tirelessly for the rights of his people from the early 1830s to his death in 1883. Meanwhile, Susan helped organize the Female Lundy Society, the first African-American women's charitable organization in the city, and would be the president of the Society for many years. Both were deeply involved in the African M.E. Church.

In the early 1840s, we find the family living on Plain Street in Albany in a building owned by Benjamin Lattimore. Lattimore was one of the earliest Albany men to attend the Colored Conventions, the first national expressions of abolition and political equality for African Americans. Lattimore was a friend of Frederick Douglass, William Lloyd Garrison, and anyone of consequence in anti-slavery and political equality movements. So it's safe to assume that the Douge family shared similar linkages to the world outside Albany.

In 1847 Mary became an assistant teacher in the segregated Wilberforce School for African children in Albany. It was here she met her first husband, Henry Hicks, who was first a teacher and then the principal of the school. Although Henry died in 1853 Mary continued to teach at the School for another six years or so.

We lose track of Mary until after the Civil War. Even though she appears to have been suffering from tuberculosis she ventured south to Virginia and South Carolina to teach children and adults under the auspices of the Freedman's Bureau, a federal organization that helped former black slaves and poor whites during Reconstruction. While in South Carolina Mary met her second husband, Andrew Williams, a carpenter, and the couple returned to Albany.

In 1880 the couple were living at 25 North Lark Street with their daughter Susie and Mary’s parents. That year the New York State Legislature enacted a the School Suffrage Law permitting women in New York to vote in school elections. Lillian Devereux Blake, the president of the New York State Women's Suffrage Association, lobbied tirelessly for the law, and she and others used it as a catalyst to establish women's suffrage societies around the state. The first meeting of the Albany Women’s Suffrage Society was held in March,1880.

The immediate goal of the Society was to get the word out about the law, and women registered to vote in the school commissioner election to be held the next month. Mary was elected a Vice President of the Society. The National Citizen and Ballot, a women's suffrage newspaper published in Syracuse, described Mary as "a stately mulatto of considerable education and refinement, “ as she lead a group of women who registered to vote in the Eleventh Ward. Mary and her mother Susan were among a group of six African American women who voted in that first school election (about 15% of the women we know voted). Mary remained a member of the Sufrage Society until her death.

On March 16, 1884, Mary passed away one month shy of her 52 birthday; she was survived by her husband and her two children. Mary was buried in Albany Rural Cemetery's Section 99 Lot 3 - the family plot. Her obituary in the The [Albany]Argus described Mary as "a notable woman, and her life was one continued effort for the advancement of her race."

The importance of Mary’s participation in the Suffrage Society, and that of her mother and friends can’t be under-estimated since it tells us that suffrage activities in Albany at the time included women of color, unlike other areas of the country.
