Sarah C. Mink (1837 - 1896) [Section 7 Lot 14]

National President of the Women’s Relief Corps, Advocated for a U.S. patriotism to be taught in the classroom

Sarah was born in Mayfield, NY, on April 7, 1837. Her paternal and maternal ancestors were early settlers of New York and sided with the revolutionaries who created the United States. Sarah married Major Charles E. Mink, a Civil War veteran and later an engineer. The two resided in Albany, Syracuse, and lastly, Watertown.

In the years following the Civil War, several civic organizations were established throughout the country to help those who survived or those who were widowed and orphaned as a result. Sarah served as President of the First Relief Corp, and later President of the State Corp. Under the request of Paul Vandervoort, commander-in-chief of the Grand Army of the Republic (G.A.R.), these organizations met in Denver in July of 1883, “to perfect a National Order, which should include within its folds loyal women from every state of the Union, who were willing to unite in a fraternity which should be on assistance to the G.A.R., in all their works of beneficence.” The organization name National Women’s Relief Corps (WRC), which was approved as an Auxiliary to the G.A.R. two years prior in Indianapolis. During this conference, this name was adopted for organizations nationwide.

The goals of the Women’s National Relief is to provide perpetually, curation and preservation of research, documents, and records that pertain to G.A.R. as well as WRC. They strive to perpetuate the memory of those who sacrificed much and sometimes all in the Great War of Rebellion (Civil War). Additionally, they assisted veterans of all wars and extended aid to widow(er)s and orphans of those affected. From 1893 – 1894, Sarah was elected the 11th National President of WRC. During her time in office, the organization adopted resolutions advocating for patriotism to be taught in public classrooms.

Sarah Mink passed away on December 3, 1896, at the age of 59 in Watertown, N.Y. of cancer. She is buried next to her husband. Her granite marker is topped with an American flag with the inscription Teach America’s children to give their heads and their hearts to God and their country. One country, one language, one flag. The bottom of her grave reads America’s highest type of noble womanhood.
