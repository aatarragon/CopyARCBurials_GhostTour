Albany House of Shelter [Section 57 Lot 7]

Reclaiming and reforming women who had strayed from the path of virtue and were living in vice

The Albany House of Shelter, commonly referred to as The House of Shelter, was located at the corner of Howard and Wendell Streets. The House was the place where women deemed delinquent, “lacking in mentality,” or “depraved by nature,” as the newspapers described one young woman in 1894, was sent for rehabilitation. Homeless women, pregnant women from the Alms House, runaways, young women abandoned by unscrupulous husbands, and girls considered unruly or wayward by their own families were confined there along with prostitutes, pickpockets, and women arrested for sundry crimes.

The House of Shelter was founded in 1868 by a group of civic-minded women led by Mrs. Samuel Pruyn. By 1872, it had moved to the corner of Howard and Wendell. The intent was to restore these fallen and lost women as useful members of society. In reality, it was a harsh place of confinement and punishment. Escapes were common. Those caught often ended up in the city jail.

Around 1900, attempts were made to reform the reformatory and make it less punishing, though it was still a harsh place. By 1915 The House went through financial problems, declining support, and outright fraud, including the misappropriation of $30,000. By the end of that year, the House of Shelter closed. The old House was torn down in the late 1950s, and the site is now part of the new Albany Convention Center.

On August 12, 1873, a deed was issued to the House of Shelter for a 768 square foot lot on the edge of the Middle Ridge in the Albany Rural Cemetery. Between July 23, 1873, and April 3, 1889, 46 women and children were buried in Lot 7, Section 57. Of them, nine were adults between the ages of 19 and 30. One has no age listed; Nettie Appleton is only identified as “Adult.” Another, Ernitine Street, was a girl of 16. The rest were children, many less than a year old. Causes of death for the women was frequently consumption, though one woman died of “decline.” Of the children, causes of death included congestion of the lungs, teething, debility, stomach, and intestinal disorders. Two children under the age of two months died of syphilis.
