Gertrude Crissey Valentine (1890 - 1919) [Section 27 Lot 16] Cenotaph

World World I YMCA Volunteer killed in France

Between 25,000 – 30,000 American women served overseas in World War I. Over 100 were women from Albany. They included nurses working at field hospitals and Base Hospital 33 (established by Albany Hospital-now Albany Med-in Portsmouth England, the Red Cross, the YWCA and the Salvation Army, many of whom worked close to combat areas. Gertrude Valentine was one of approximately 125 American women who died in the line of duty during the Great War.

Gertrude Crissey Valentine was born on April 8, 1890, to Clarence and Laura Valentine. She lived at 80 Chestnut Street in Albany. Her father, Clarence Valentine, was a partner in a firm making such wooden products as packing boxes, moldings, shingles, as well as felt weather stripping.

She attended the State College for Teachers’ Model School, then went on to Vassar College where she graduated in 1913. Gertrude returned to Albany and continued her education at the State College with plans for a teaching career. Gertrude was involved in local musical societies as newspapers mention young Miss Valentine playing the piano and coronet at various social functions.

During World War I, she served as a volunteer for the YMCA, first in England and later France. At the close of the War, while still serving as a canteen worker, she was killed in an automobile accident on June 11, 1919. She is buried in Plot C, Row 7, Grave 13, at the Oise-Aisne American Cemetery, Fere-en-Tardenois, France. At Albany Rural Cemetery, a cenotaph was placed in her honor within the William T. Valentine family plot.
