Church Grounds [Section 49 Lots 1-14]

Reinternments from Albany’s various city burying grounds

The cemetery’s Church Grounds contains only 1,060 headstones, and it is widely known that the placement of these headstones does not coincide with the actual burial location of the name(s) inscribed thereon. But how many people were actually reinterred from Washington Park and various other church yard cemeteries has been disputed for decades

.

In 1868 the City of Albany removed “the neglected dead” from the State Street Buying Ground. The mass disinterment was a step toward the creation of Washington Park. The old Burying Ground had been in use from about 1800 until it was closed by law in 1867 “for the keeping, burial or deposit of human remains. Prior to the closure, new burials there had dramatically declined with most new internments now taking place at newer cemeteries like the Albany Rural and its later neighbor, St. Agnes.

Before the Common Council contracted one A. J. Phillips to exhume and relocate the remains from the burial grounds, some families had privately removed the graves of loved ones to new plots a Albany Rural Cemetery.

But just how many graves were removed to make way for Washington Park?

Almost all modern accounts of the Park’s sepulchral past cite a figure of 40,000 bodies, but suspicions rank that total as far too high.

What might be the first reference to a total of 40,000 comes from the Albany Chronicles compiled by city historian Cuyler Reynolds. The Chronicles were published in 1906, nearly forty years after the mass disinterment. This figure appears twice, once in a photograph caption and once in an entry which reads “Rural Cemetery internments number 78,081 (40,000 of which were brought from burial ground, site of Washington Park).”

The source Reynolds used for this figure is unknown. Reynolds Chronicles is a valuable resource, but it is not without errors. It is possible that the figure “40,000” was the result of a mistake in transcribing an older hand written source.

Whatever the origin of the number, the figure has been repeated many times over the years. A 1951 pictorial review on the history of Washington Park by C.R. Rosenberry mentions “in order for Washington Park to serve the living, 40,000 dead people had to be moved elsewhere.” Presumably, such articles were written using Reynold’s Chronicles as a source.

Recently, a document from the Albany Institute if History and Art has been transcribed which contradicts the 40,000 figure. The Report of the Committee On Removal of Old Cemeteries In Albany – 1868 contains interesting details on the special committee tasked to “dissenter, remove, and reinter the remains of the neglected dead. The document includes the following observation on the number of graves to be removed:

Your committee, from what they believe to be accurate information, and from their own observation, estimate that the number to be removed will be from 11,000 to 14,000. Basing their calculations on that estimate, your committee ha ordered 8,000 boxes in which to deposit the remains to be made. They deem it advisable not to order a large number than that at present, thinking it is safer to be largely under their estimate than to be anything over. The price of each box is one dollar, which your committee think is very reasonable.

In the end nearly 950 additional boxes must have been ordered, as the final cost for the boxes was $8947.00. Even allowing for multiple remains in some boxes, it still would be unlikely the total would approach 40,000.

In addition, the report specifies the dimensions of the new graves to be opened in the newly purchased “Church Grounds” as 2 feet six inches by 4 feet, or 10 square feet allocated to each new grave. To accommodate 40,000 graves of this size, an area of 400,000 square feet, or something in excess of 9 acres, would be required. Yet, the Church Ground lot purchased to receive the “neglected dead” measures only an acre and a half.

There is also the issue of time. The committee noted that the contractor, A.J. Pjillips, was averaging removals of 100 to 150 bodies a day. At that rate, it would have taken between 266 and 400 days to complete the transfer. A.J. Phillips had begun the transfer process around August 1868, and by October, had completed the work. That time frame is much more in keeping with the 10,000 to 14,000 graves.
