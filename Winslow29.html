John Flack Winslow (1858-1877) [Section 65 Lot 39]

:

Financier for the U.S.S. Monitor, 5th President of Rensselaer Polytechnic Institute

John Flack Winslow was born on November 5, 1810, to Mary Corning, and Captain Richard Winslow, an ironmaster and descendant of Governor Winslow of the Plymouth Colony. The family resided in Bennington Vermont until 1815 when they moved to Albany.

At age 17, he left his schooling and worked for two years as a clerk at the merchant house W. & A. Marvin Company, later moving to New York City and New Orleans. Following in his father’s footsteps, In 1831 he took a manager position with New Jersey Iron Company. Three years later he purchased an iron company in Bergen County, NJ operating it until 1837 when Erastus Corning approached him to work at the Albany Nail Factory.

Winslow ran the daily operations while Corning acquired rail lines which eventually became New York Central Railroad. To stay competitive in the market, Winslow traveled to Europe and purchased rights to “cutting-edge iron and steel” technology. He partnered with Corning on other business ventures, such as the Lulworth Iron Company in Mount Savage, M.D., where he served as president.

In 1861, he and John Griswold worked with inventor John Ericsson to construct the U.S.S. Monitor, the ironclad warship to combat the Confederate’s warship, Virginia, also known as the Merrimac. Winslow already had contracts with the U.S. government, and he helped with the construction of, the Galena. After lobbying President Lincoln the project was approved on October 4, 1861 by the Secretary of the Navy. Despite approval for funding, Winslow agreed to finance the project himself and received occasional reimbursements from the U.S. Navy.

Under the direction of Winslow, much of the work was constructed in Troy including the revolving gun turret which sat on top of the ship which could rotate separately from the ship. In March of 1862, the U.S.S. Monitor was in the Battle of Hampton Roads and credited with saving most of the Navy during that battle.

Winslow purchased the manufacturing rights to Bessemer Steel in 1864, the first inexpensive industrial process for the mass production of iron to steel.

Winslow’s first wife, Nancy Beach Jackson, died in December of 1862, whom he had married in September of 1832. Though he did not have any children with her, there is an inscription that he may have adopted his brother’s son. In 1867 he married Harriet Wickes of Albany and had two girls, though one died in childhood. He adopted the nephew of Harriet after the death of his mother shortly after giving birth in 1883.

From 1865 - 1868 he was made the fifth president of Rensselaer Polytechnic Institute. What made this notable was that he was the first person to take this position and not be a minister. Winslow Chemical Laboratory was named in his honor. After serving as president, he returned to his estate along the Hudson River in Poughkeepsie, Woodcliff. His estate eventually became the theme park, Woodcliff Pleasure Park which marketed itself as “The Worlds Most Beautiful Amusement Park.” It was constructed by Fred Ponty who also built Paradise Park in Rye, later to became Rye Playland. The park was in operation from 1927 – 1941. Today the property is the northernmost part of Marist College.

Winslow died on March 10, 1892, at the age of 81. He is interred in the John F. Winslow vault located near the chapel off Cemetery Ave. Also interred are his parents, his first and second wife, as well as many of his siblings and direct relatives.
