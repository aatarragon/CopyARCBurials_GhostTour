Harriet Alfrata Chapman Thompson (1871-1922) [Section 99 Lot 61]

Chief Stenographer of NYS Library Catalog Department under Melvil Dewey, author of a novel which has been rediscovered by African American and feminist literary circles, possibly first African American to graduate from Albany Business College

The Chapman family came to Albany from New Jersey with their children while the Civil War was raging for reasons we’ve yet to discover. But we do know that New Jersey was not a particular hospitable state for African Americans, it was the only Northern state that didn’t vote for Abraham Lincoln in 1860. Additionally, New Jersey was the only Northern state which failed to ratify the 13th, 14th, and 15th civil rights amendments after the Civil War. Although slavery was theoretically abolished in 1840, there were still people enslaved until after the War in 1865. In the early 1860s, New Jersey politics was rife with Confederate sympathizers.

At the time of the Chapman’s arrival, the family consisted of John Chapman, his wife Harriet Alfarata, and five children, William, John, Thomas, Charles, and Arabella. In 1863 John’s occupation was listed in the city directory as a waiter at the Delavan House, which was known to hire free African Americans and runaway slaves, with the family living in Arbor Hill. Shortly thereafter, the family moved to Hamilton Street below Lark St., where they remained for decades. The move made it easier for the children to attend the one segregated school for black children on Chestnut Street, the Wilberforce School.

However, this changed shortly after Harriet’s birth in 1871. In 1873 the Albany Board of Public Instruction desegregated Albany schools. For years the African American community, including John Chapman, petitioned and lobbied the city to desegrate. In 1873 things came to a head. First there was a lawsuit against the Board by a black parent, William Dietz, who lived in Arbor Hill. In April the New York Legislature passed the Civil Rights Act of 1873 that banned racial desicrimination in schools, public transporation and other venues. It’s back against the wall, the School Board relented. And so Harriet’s older sister, Arabella, became the first African American child to graduate from Albany High School in 1877.

Harriet attended an integrated public grammar school (Public School 2 on Chestnut Street -previously the Wilberforce School building) and went on to graduate from Albany High School in 1889. It’s quite is possible that she was the first African American woman to graduate (1891) from the Albany Business College on North Pearl St.

Initially Harriet worked for The Pure Baking Powder Company, but then entered New York State government as a stenographer in the New York State Library in 1893. Gone were the days when black women were relegated to manual labor or service positions. Harriet was employed directly by Melvil Dewey, Chief Librarian (and creator of the Dewey Decimal System). She worked in the Library for over 25 years; at the time of her retirement Harriet was the chief stenography of the cataloging department.

In 1921, at the age of 50, Harriet married John W. Thompson. Thompson was a charismatic and influential somewhat older man. He was born in Virginia, and came to Rochester, N.Y. probably in the 1870s. By the 1880s he was a waiter in the elegant Powers Hotel in that city. He first became well-known as the captain of the Powers Flyaway Baseball Club composed of the black waiters of the Hotel.

In 1894 Thompson first suggested that the “colored” members of the Rochester’s Masonic Lodges erect a statute as a tribute to Frederick Douglass. It was an idea that caught on across across the city and state, and he became head of the committee to erect the statue (which became a memorial when Douglass died in 1895). He raised money and petitioned the NYS Legislature for funding. His book about the experience "An authentic history of the Douglass monument; biographical facts and incidents in the life of Frederick Douglass" was published in 1903.

As a result of his effeorts he becme well known in political circles. For the next 30 years he worked for New York State government, the Republican Party and played an important role in African American politics in New York State and nationwide. Consequently, he spent time in Albany on a regular basis,which is how we assume he met Harriet.

Their wedding was held in Catskill in St. Luke;s, a small Episcopal Church. Her best friend of 40 years, Helena Goines, served as maid of honor. (Helena lived in New York City where she was a teacher in the public school system.)

Sadly they were married less than a year when Hairriet died suddenly in 1922 while visiting her brother William in Cambridge, Mass. At the time of her death, Melvil Dewey sent his condolences and said of Harriet she was “ a woman of unusual ability and character, who always commanded my utmost respect and confidence.”

James loved Harriet so much that after her death, he published her novel, Idealia – a Utopian Dream or Resthaven in 1923. It’s a tale of a charming and simple small world in which all people live in harmony and work for the greater good. Recently Idealia has been re-discovered in the world of feminist and African American literature. The book is available for free on google e-books and Internet Archive.

John died in 1926 and is buried next to Harriet.
