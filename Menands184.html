Home for Aged Men [Section 121 Lot 335, Section 104 Lot 668]

Group plot for residents of Menands Manor

The Home For Aged Men was located at the corner of Broadway and McDonald Circle in Menands. Initially built for Joseph Strain in 1790, it was used as their summer residence. Strain was an owner of a candle factory at 54 Church Street in Albany. Strain’s son, daughter, and sisters are known to be the first bodies buried at Albany Rural Cemetery in May of 1845.

On October 5, 1876, the Home for Aged Men was incorporated after citizens of Troy and Albany had voiced their concern about the lack of “sympathetic care for its aged needy,” such as men who had served in the Civil War. In 1877 the building and surrounding property were purchased from Mrs. Harriet D. Perry, widow of Theodore Perry of Albany, with renovations performed during the following years. The building had additions on either side, creating a “U shape,” with the exterior altered in the Stick / Eastlake style, including a slate roof and gables, thanks in part to James Barclay Jermain, who also established the Fairview Home of the friendless in Watervliet.

The property gained the name, Menands Manor, and in the 1920s, George C. Hawley donated the funds in memory of his wife, Theodora “Dora” Hawley, to the building. In 1925 a 2-story brick addition was added to the south side of the building to accommodate more residents, an infirmary, and two sunrooms.

While in operation, many distinguished men of the Capital Region resided in Menands Manor. The 1974 50th Anniversary booklet of the Village of Menands describes the trustees and officers of overseeing the daily operations as a “Who’s Who of the areas,” including many well connected and philanthropic women. One such individual was Adele Menand, who served as superintendent from 1901 until her death in 1911. She was the daughter of Louis Menand, namesake to the village, and is said to have rented land on what later became part of the property.

With the creation of Social Security, Menands Manor went into a decline, and in 1975 women were allowed to reside out of “financial necessity.” The building was sold in the mid-1990s to the Center for the Disabled. Today it houses the New York Council of Nonprofits, Inc. at 272 Broadway, Menands, NY 12204

Group plots were purchased for members of the home who did not have a dedicated spot in a family burial plot. The first group plot sold for $1 in Section 121 Lot 335 on October 15, 1879. A total of 37 individuals are buried in this lot. The first on September 22, 1880, was David Kniskern, aged 75 years; the last one occurred on October 25, 1924, to Henry Watson, age 64 years. A second group plot was purchased on September 20, 1923, also for $1. The first burial took place on December 10, 1924, to Edward Lockwood, age 77 years. The most recent burial took place on May 6, 2012, for Edward Kosiba, who died on December 25, 2012, at the age of 84. Thirty eight individuals are buried in the second lot.
