Alfred Billings Street (1810-1881) [Section 37, Lot 23]

Albany Attorney, State Librarian and Poet

Alfred Billings Street was born 1811 in Poughkeepsie, New York and raised in Monticello where he read law in his father's office. He relocated to Albany in 1839 where he married Miss Elizabeth Weed. Street practiced law for only three years before devoting himself to a literary career. He spent a year editing The Northern Light, a journal "devoted to free discussion, and to the diffusion of useful knowledge, miscellaneous literature and general intelligence."

Alfred authored several prose works based on expeditions into the Adirondack wilderness, "Woods and Waters, or the Saranac and the Racket" (1860), "The Indian Pass (1869), and "Lake and Mountain; or Autumn in the Adirondacks" (1870). He is primarily remembered, though, for his poetry, much of which focused on natural or historical themes such as "The Burning of Schenectady" and ""Frontenac: or the Atotarho of the Iroquois." During the Civil War, his highly sentimental and patriotic poems were a staple in periodicals and were included in his two volume "The Poems of Alfred B. Street."

Of his work, Edgar Alan Poe remarked that "Mr. Street has fine taste, and a keen sense of the beautiful. He writes carefully, elaborately, and correctly" and Henry Wadsworth Longfellow described him as "without exception, the best delineator of forest scenery in America."

Alfred also served as the State Librarian from 1842 to 1868 and as law librarian until 1868. Alfred Billings Street died at his Dove Street home on June 2, 1881. He was 70 years old and had suffered from chronic Brights disease. He was buried in the "Greenleaf Forest" area of the Cemetery. Street's grave has no headstone, but he rests next to his wife, Elizabeth, and his name is carved on her stone. His burial card includes the notation that he was "a poet."

Alfred Street was often called upon to write poems for civic events such as the dedication of the cornerstone of the Saratoga Battle Monument. He wrote a poem for the consecration of the new Rural Cemetery and recited the work during the ceremony on October 7, 1844. The ceremony, which was attended by a great crowd which included numerous local dignitaries and private citizens, was held in a natural amphitheater within the ravine between the South and Middle Ridges. For many years, a path leading through the ravine near Consecration Lake and the site of the ceremony was known as Rhymer's Glen.
