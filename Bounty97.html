Bounty Jumpers (1864) [Section 98, Tier (Row) 12]

Four unknown Union soldiers found dead along the Railroad tracks.

During the Civil War, men would enlist for money referred to as the “Bounty”; a program of cash bonuses paid to entice enlistees into the army. The system was much abused, particularly during the Civil War. During the period of 1861 to 1865, the federal government, along with states and localities, paid about $750,000,000 in recruitment bounties. Congress authorized a $100 bounty in July 1861 to men enlisting for three years. Bounty-jumping is the term applied to the widespread practice of enlisting, collecting the bonus, deserting, reenlisting, collecting another bonus, while never actually serving as soldiers, or participating in battles. Desertion was an offence punishable by death

The Albany Morning Express newspaper for Saturday June 11, 1864 published a news story titled: “THE DEAD BOUNTY JUMPERS”. The context continued:

“The bodies of four dead soldiers who were found lying near the track of the Boston Railroad, were taken to the Barracks, and will be kept for a few days for identification. They were all examined. The limbs of some of them were broken, and the bodies were all terribly torn and mangled. In the pocket of one was found $173, on another $70, and on the other two nothing. Several suspicious young men followed the bodies to the Barracks, but they pretended not to know them, nor could they be identified by their comrades who were sufferers from bounty jumping, but who were able to look at them”.

The true identities of the four soldiers was never ascertained.

Cemetery records document in Section 98, “A space of twelve feet square at the end of this tier (Row) 12 is occupied by the remains of soldiers who were killed by escaping from (Railroad) cars in attempting to Jump-The-Bounty” The actual gravesite is unmarked but likely at the North end of Tier 12
