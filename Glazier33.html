Willard Glazier (1841-1905)[Section 29 Lot 26]

Civil War Captain. Confederate Prisoner of War. Author, explorer, and namesake to Lake Glazier.

Willard Worchester Glazier was born in Fowler, St. Lawrence County, N.Y. on August 22, 1841. He was the eldest of eight children born to Ward Glazer and Mehitable C. Bolton.

Like his father before him, Glazier attended the Gouverneur Wesleyan Seminary in St. Lawrence County. When school was not in session, he made money as a trapper in the North County. He saved enough money to move to Albany in 1859 to attend the New York State Normal School, also know as the Albany Normal School. This was a state-sponsored teachers college, which would later become the State University of New York (SUNY) at Albany.

The two year program at the Albany Normal School was the only opportunity for students to extend their education with attendees coming from rural areas of northern and western New York State. Glazier was pursuing a profession as a teacher but ran out of money after one semester. He found work as a teacher in Schodack in 1859-60 which helped him return to school to finish his degree. But there was an intervening national event – the War of the Rebellion.

In August 1861 Glazier enlisted in the 2nd New York Volunteer Cavalry, known as the Harris Light Cavalry regiment. Glazier was accepted quickly into the cavalry because he owned a horse. He was mustered in as private upon enlistment, then soon appointed sergeant and later promoted to 2nd lieutenant. On October 18, 1863, nears Buckland Mills, Virginia , Glazier was captured and became a Confederate prisoner of war. He was sent to Libby Prison in Richmond, Virginia, which by that time was a prison for officers only. Glazier engaged in an escape attempt, only to be re-captured by his captors. Glazier and the remaining prisoners were then transferred to Georgia for temporary confinement.

He was transferred again to Charleston, and later to Columbia, South Carolina. In Columbia, he attempted another escape, but was re-captured once again. On his third attempt he would successfully escape with the assistance of African-Americans. Finally back in Union lines, Glazier returned to Albany in December 1864. As his initial term of enlistment had expired, Glazier re-enlisted in February 1865 as a 1st lieutenant in the 26th New York Cavalry for the final two months of the war.

After the war Glazier gave frequent lectures about his war experiences. In 1865 local printer, publisher and author, Joel Munsell, encouraged Glazier to use the notes made during captivity to write his memoirs. The result was his first book, The Capture, the Prison-Pen, and the Escape, published in 1865 - a biographical account of his 14-month imprisonment. The book sold over 400,000 copies and became known as one of the bestselling, non-fiction books at the time. At one point it sold more copies than Uncle Tom’s Cabin by Harriet Beecher Stowe.

In 1868 Glazier married Harriet Ayers of Cincinnati. They kept house in Albany. Their union produced daughter, Alice. They would later adopt another daughter, Helen.

Glazier wrote several more books, including Three Years in the Federal Cavalry (1870); Battles for the Union (1874); Ocean to Ocean by Horseback (1876); Heroes of Three Wars (1878); Peculiarities of American Cities (1883); and Down the Great River (1887). In addition to his writings Glazier became an explorer and he documented his travels. His 1876 book Ocean to Ocean by Horseback is a narrative description of a journey to California. He began his expedition in Boston and culminated in San Francisco.

In 1881 he traveled via canoe up the Mississippi River, starting in Louisiana. During his travels, he claimed to discover a small lake south of Lake Itasca in Minnesota. Though it was already on maps from government surveys, Glazier concluded that Elk Lake was the true source of the Mississippi River, and he campaigned in 1887 to rename Elk Lake to Lake Glazier. This did not bode well with the Minnesota Historical Society who would investigate his findings. Glazier confessed that he had not visited any streams feeding into Elk Lake. To solidify Lake Itasca as the true source of the Mississippi, the Minnesota Legislature passed a resolution, so their “earliest explorers be not robbed of their just laurels.”

At the start of the Spanish-American War, Glazier organized a provisional regiment of Illinois Infantry called the Blue & Gray Legion of Chicago. He served as Colonel of the regiment, but the unit was not sent to Cuba. In 1902, and then again in 1903, he would explore uncharted territory on the Labrador Peninsula. His exploring in northern Maine would result in his discovery of what is now known as Glazier Lake along U.S. / Canadian border north of St. John’s River.

Willard Glazier passed away in Albany on April 26, 1905 at the age of 64 of heart disease, five years after he and his wife adopted Helen. He was interred at Albany Rural Cemetery in a section that is known as Millionaires Row. Glazier’s monument is a large granite marker marked with his military accomplishments, crossed swords, a bugle, and an American flag. His life also led to the 1884 biography by author John A. Owens, entitled Sword, and Pen.
