Thomas Elkins (1818 – 1900) [Section 100 Lot 97]

Albany's Renaissance Man: Doctor, Dentist, Druggist, Inventor, Abolitionist, Community and Political Activist

To learn more about Thomas Elkins involvement with Abolition click the link below

More Info on Thomas Elkins & Abolition.

Thomas Elkins was one of the most fascinating African-American men in Albany in the 19th century. He was born about 1819 in New York City and came to Albany with his parents in the 1820s. In his early teens, Elkins served as an apprentice to the druggist Herman Wynkoop at Wynkoop's shop at Broadway and Maiden Lane and lived in Wynkoop's home at 14 Orange Street. Following his apprenticeship with Dr. Wynkoop, he studied dentistry with a local dentist. (His obituary said they were associated in practice in Montreal and then Saratoga.)

Unlike other local African American men in Albany of the time, Elkins was not opposed to the colonization movement. In 1847 at the age of 28, he sailed to Liberia under the auspices of the Maryland Colonization Society. Frederick Douglass' newspaper The North Star reported in 1848 that he was also serving as a school superintendent as well as practicing dentistry.

Upon his return, he entered into the study of medicine with Dr. Alden March, founder of Albany Medical College, and another professor, Dr. Thomas Hun. (There's a reference in The North Star to a student from Liberia, c. 1850, studying at the College – we believe that is Dr. Elkins.)

By 1850 Elkins is listed as a practicing dentist at 188 Lumber Street, now Livingston Avenue, home of his stepfather, John Butler; his mother, Sarah; and his half-sisters. By1852, he's set up his shop at 84 North Swan Street, around the corner, and he's still living at home with his mother, who has become a widow. In 1855 he moved his apothecary shop to 790 Broadway and remained for decades in the same general location. It was about this time he was appointed by the City of Albany to be the district physician in the 3rd ward and would continue in that position until 1870.

During this time, he became politically active and was identified as the Secretary/Treasurer of the Vigilance Committee tasked with raising funds for the Underground Railroad (UGRR). During the Civil War, Elkins was appointed by Massachusetts Governor John Andrew as a medical examiner for the recruits for the 54th Massachusetts "colored" regiment (you know it from the movie 'Glory), and the 55th regiment created to handle the overflow influx of African-American recruits.

Just after the war, Elkin's mother died and moved his residence to 67 Second Street near North Swan Street, while at the same time becoming social and politically active. He attended the New York State Colored Convention in Albany in 1866 and became the Vice President of the newly formed African American Literary Society (for men only), immersed himself in Republican politics as the 15th amendment granted African American men the right to vote in 1870. Additionally, he was part of a coalition to pressure the Albany Board of Education to integrate the High School and was an active member of the County Dental Society.

And he tinkers. Over about a decade, he patents three inventions; the first was a quilting/ironing table. The second invention was the most splendiferous commode you've ever seen - a veritable throne. His final patent was for the technology of one of the earliest refrigeration units (patent number 221,222 in 1879).

And over the next two decades, his was a life well-lived. He continued to practice and participate in the social and activities of the African –American Albany (he's the first African –American to serve on a federal grand jury in Albany County).

Dr. Thomas Elkins died on August 10, 1900, at the age of 82. His funeral was held at the Cathedral of All-Saints and was thronged with attendees, where his pallbearers were the sons of Francis Van Vranken, his closest friend – a barber – who had been a member of the UGRR. One of the newspaper obituaries makes it quite clear that, but for his race, he would have become a licensed physician, although he was treated as if he was by most of Albany, including the police and the courts. "Prejudice alone at his color has prevented him from making a competence at his profession, as he is in the opinion of many competent to judge, one of the ablest physicians and dentists of this or any other age, either in this city or elsewhere."
