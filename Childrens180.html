Corning Foundation for Christian Work [Section 121 Lot 313]

Burial group plot for patients of the Children’s Hospital and St. Margaret’s Home for Infants

Erastus Corning Jr. followed in his father’s footsteps in business and philanthropy. He served as president of the Albany Iron Works, Albany City National Bank, Albany Cemetery Association (Albany Rural Cemetery), and director of the New York Central and Hudson River Railroad under the leadership of Cornelius Vanderbilt.

In 1872, a couple of months before the death of his father, he established the Corning Foundation for Christian Work in the Diocese of Albany. This foundation expanded the work of the Episcopal Church in Albany by establishing St. Agnes School, help fund the building of the Cathedral of All Saints on Swan Street between Elk and Lafayette, which sits on the site of a former iron foundry established by Erastus Corning, I.

The foundation also established a Children’s Hospital on the corner of Hawk and Elk Street. Opening in 1875, this benevolent institution dealt with children, ages 1 – 15 suffering from chronic and other diseases. The Children’s Hospital, which had 90 beds, was operated by the Sisterhood of the Holy Child Jesus, who also operated St. Agnes School and St. Margaret’s Home for Babies, which cared for “destitute, homeless or motherless infants and young children.” Situated between St. Agnes and the Children’s Hospital, these “institutions of learning and mercy that will gather about it” was a vision of the Right Reverend Bishop William Crosswell Doane, first Bishop of the Episcopal Diocese of Albany.

A final resting place was established at Albany Rural Cemetery for those of the Children’s Hospital and St. Margaret’s Home for Babies who died while in their care. Two plots were purchased for $1 each, first on October 15, 1879 (513 feet), and then April 10, 1890 (627 feet). The first burial was Henry La March, died July 21, 1882, age one year six months, and the last Jane Quinn, died February 17, 1934, age one year two months, 12 days. In total, about 720 are buried with a majority between 0 - 1 year. There is a 17-year-old by the name of Edward Harrison Franklin, who died on April 27, 1895, Woodrow Wilson, died May 21, 1919, and Baby Chase, who lived 3 hours, June 19, 1911. There is one adult, Miss Ellen G. Gibson, who was born in Keef Scotland and died in New York City at the age of 44 on May 16, 1928.

By the 1930s, these organizations began to break up as several buildings were threatened to slide into Sheridan Hollow. St. Agnes moved to Loudonville, which operated until 1975 when it merged with the Roman Catholic Kenwood Academy, now known as The Doane Stuart School. St. Margaret’s Home moved to property on the former Almshouse, which had moved to the area near the present-day Albany International Airport. By 1967 it was a nursing facility that cared for chronically ill infants and children, and by 1999 it integrated into the Center for Disability Services located on Hackett Boulevard under a similar name, St. Margaret’s Center.

The Children’s Hospital remained on Elk Street but sold the property in 1959 to New York State, and razed in 1960 slated to become a 727 car garage. The hospital moved to Hackett Boulevard under the name Good Samaritan Center, across from St. Paul’s Episcopal Church, which operated from October 1961 until 1999 when the Diocese of Albany sold it to Albany Medical Center. It is now the site of their south clinical campus.
