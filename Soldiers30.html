Soldiers & Sailors Lot [Section 75 Lot 7]

Burial site of 149 Civil War Veterans

The Solider’s and Sailor’s Lot was created in 1862 when the trustees of Albany Rural Cemetery donated a 0.16-acre lot to the Federal Government for “a sufficient and suitable ground be set apart to inter the remains of officers and soldiers who have fallen or may fall in endeavoring to suppress the present rebellion.” Those interred here all served during the Civil War. Many of the soldiers buried here died in local military hospitals, although some had been transferred from graves at Southern battlefields. Some of these graves were provided at no cost for soldiers whose families could not afford to bury them in private plots.

A total of 149 Civil War soldiers and sailors are buried here, the last burial occurring in 1897. The graves consist of orderly rows of white marble stones which face the only monument on the lot, a 15-ft tall Grand Army of the Republic (GAR) memorial. Erected in 1870, it includes a life sized Union soldier at the top, in the position of “In Place – Rest.” The four bronze plates adorn each of the four sides of the monument carry the names of 648 Albany residents who died in the Rebellion. The plaques were cast from cannons used during the war, obtained by Hon. Eli Perry, former Mayor of Albany and member of the U.S. House of Representatives. The monument also includes two oval bas reliefs of Abraham Lincoln on the east and west sides of the memorial.

Grave No. 6 is the burial location of Pvt. Martin Buck, Co. A, 34th NY Infantry (a.k.a. the Herkimer Regiment). Pvt. Buck was from West Troy (present day Watervliet) and was killed at the Battle of Antietam in September 1862.

One of those buried here served in the 69th NY Infantry, part of the Irish Brigade, and another with the 164th NY Infantry, part of the Irish Legion. Founded in 1861 and 1862, they were Union Army brigades which were almost exclusively Irish immigrants from New York, Massachusetts, and Pennsylvania. One member of the 69th NY is Pvt. Bernard Trainor (Grave 40) who enlisted in August of 1862, a month before the regiment fought at Antietam. He was severely wounded at Gettysburg and was discharged for disability.

Irishmen also buried in the Soldier's & Sailor’s Lot are Musician Joseph Maguire (Grave 95), member of the 63rd NY Infantry of the Irish Brigade who was wounded at Malvern Hill and Petersburg; Corp. Richard Jefferson (Grave 79), 170th NY Infantry; and Pvt. Michael Curtain (Grave 62), 164th NY Infantry, both of the Irish Legion.

The tour theme titled Soldiers Lot reveals the headstone identities of all 149 veterans interred in the Soldiers and Sailors Lot.

In 2013 and 2014 the Soldier’s & Sailor’s Lot, which is overseen by Saratoga National Cemetery, was cleaned and headstones re-leveled for uniformity.

This site does not include Negro soldiers. However, there are three “colored troops” interred in the GAR Plot in Section 100A, Lot 100, near the western edge of the North Ridge. Four other colored troops buried in Albany Rural were part of the famous 54th Massachusetts Infantry, the first all black regiment in the Union Army (excpting that all officers were white.) The 54th Massachusetts is best known for their attack on Fort Wagner, South Carolina, which was highlighted in the 1989 film Glory starring Matthew Broderick, Morgan Freeman and Denzel Washington.

Over one thousand men that served during the Civil War are buried throughout the 467 acres of Albany Rural Cemetery. Of those, six were awarded the Medal of Honor, 26 were generals, and three served in the Confederate Army.
